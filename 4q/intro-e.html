The 4Q Go Game Format

The 4Q Go Game Format was conceived for the sole purpose of facilitating human reading and writing of Go game records. For instance, the four San-San poins are simply A3, B3, C3, and D3; or, if you prefer, A33, etc. For the real impatient, jump right in to this demo, which shows everything.

I. Nine Locations, Multiple Landmarks, and Relative Positions

A more formal name of the 4Q Go Game Format would be "four-quadrant Go record keeping". Further more, it can also be characterized as "nine locations, multiple landmarks, and relative positions". Essentially, it partitions the board into nine areas, so that mentally visulizing positions becomes much more intuitive than a simplistic XY coordinates system. Better yet, because of its relative nature, all the meaningful connotations of the positions (San-San is a perfect example) are retained and reflected by the symbols. Thanks to the myriads of natural landmark points, nearly half the positions need no XY coordinates, which greatly reduces the burden of mental mapping.

Go game record keeping by quadrants is not a new idea at all. However, the method introduced here appears to be close to optimal. There are most likely similar schemes available, which have not been explored and researched extensively, and this work is totally original. This would not be a huge crime by any means, nor is it an earth-shattering breakthough of any kind. Yet, its usefulness, effectiveness and aesthetics turn out to be very inspiring and rewarding!

II. The Basic Structure

The nine partitions of a board are: four 9-by-9 quadrants, four center lines (between quadrants), and Tengen. Incidentally, each partition is identified by one of the nine stars on the board.

  1. The four quadrants, named A, B, C, and D, starts from the lower-left corner of the player playing black, and progresses clockwise.
    • The coordinates are typical mathematical tuples of XY, originating from the tip of the corner, whose value is 1.
    • The diagonals have the same X and Y values, the coordinates need just one digit. E.g. B22 equates to B2.
  2. The four side stars are named East, West, North and South, facing the player playing black, noted with the first letter of each.
    • The coordinates need just a single digit, starting from 1 which is on the edge of the board.
  3. Tengen is noted as O.
  4. The nine stars themselves are noted with their respective letters without any digits, but it is not a crime to append 4 or 44 to it.

III. More Landmark Points

The nine stars are deemed essential landmarks of the board. Yet it is still too big and sparse, that counting grids to ascertain positions is boring, tedious and error-prone. Hence, a group of secondary landmark points are elected, and they are the mid points between adjacent stars. See the diagram to the right.

For instance, the mid point between stars A and S is noted as AS; between A and W, AW; and between A and O, AO. Genius!

Thusly, we have identified 9 top-tier and 16 second-tier landmark points, totalling 25.

IV. Neighboring Positions

For each landmark position, its four neighbors are given special treatment as well. They are noted with the landmark position annotated with ^, <, >, and v, respectively.
